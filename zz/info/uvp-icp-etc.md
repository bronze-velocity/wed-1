Not a template. Not Kahoot with your names dropped in. A custom-built interactive app where every question, every story beat, every prompt, every bingo square was written by you — or built from what you told us. Guests use it on their phones. The room comes alive. You keep the output forever.

## Ideal Customer Profile

### Primary: The Couple

**Demographics:** 27–38. Probably a dual-income couple, combined $100k+ household. Already spending $20k–$60k on the wedding.

**Psychographics:**
- Describes their wedding as "not typical" / "very us" / "more of a party than a ceremony"
- Experiences-over-things buyer. Has spent money on a trip instead of a TV.
- Design-aware. Notices when something looks generic. Has opinions about fonts.
- Social proof-conscious — cares that guests have a great time, not just a fine time.
- Has probably already described their wedding with a specific vibe: dark romance, European summer, cozy cabin, rooftop NYC, etc.

**The internal monologue they have before finding Wepho:**
> "We want our wedding to feel like us. Not like every other wedding. I don't want guests sitting at tables on their phones being bored. I want people to actually talk to each other. I want something they'll remember. But I don't have time to build an app."

**What triggers the purchase:**
- They see a specific app idea and picture their guests using it. The "that would be so us" moment.
- Someone at a recent wedding described something like this and they've been trying to find it since.
- A planner or vendor mentioned it.

**What blocks the purchase:**
- $2,000 feels like a lot in the abstract — resolved by the price reframe (flowers, photographer, caterer).
- Fear that guests won't use it — resolved by social proof and the "even 30% makes the wall alive" framing.
- Worry about technical failure — resolved by clear reliability language and hand-off framing ("we handle it, you just scan the QR").

**Guest list size sweet spot:** 60–200. Below 60, the live wall and competitive dynamics feel sparse. Above 200, it still works but the intimacy of "this is about us" scales fine.

### Secondary: The Wedding Planner

**Who they are:** Planners specializing in destination, luxury, or design-forward weddings. They have clients who are in the primary ICP above. They are looking for differentiated experiences to offer. They influence 3–10 wedding purchases per year.

**What makes them recommend:**
- Zero extra work for them. Wepho handles everything.
- No download required (they've been burned by apps that confused older guests).
- It reflects well on them — the couple credits them with finding something special.
- It's not a platform that sells to their clients or competes with their relationships.

**What makes them hesitate:**
- They've never heard of this category. The pitch must create it.
- They don't know who runs the app on the day (answer: the couple/MC via a simple moderation screen; the planner touches nothing).
- They're risk-averse — a failed recommendation hurts their reputation.

**Entry point:** Destination wedding planners first. They have the highest coordination burden (multi-day events, guests traveling internationally) and the highest-budget couples.

## The Outcomes Worth Selling (not the features)

These are the moments and feelings — the things to lead with in copy.

**The room coming alive.** Guests who arrived as strangers to each other, now comparing bingo cards. Tables that hadn't spoken in 20 minutes now debating who wins the roast board. The cocktail hour that was awkward for 10 minutes and then wasn't.

**The couple finally telling their story properly.** The groom's work colleagues who never knew how they met, now genuinely riveted. The trivia question everyone got wrong. The chapter of the scavenger hunt that made someone cry.

**The moment the live wall fills up.** The first submission appears on the projector. Then three more. Then the room notices. Then everyone's on their phone at once — because they want to be on the wall too.

**The grandmother's video message.** She records a 45-second message sealed for the 10th anniversary. She might not be there when they open it. Both of them know this when they watch it in year 10. That's not a feature. That's a product that matters.

**Opening the keepsake a week later.** The emotion graph showing the spike during the vows. The PDF of advice from 80 people who love them. The compilation of parallel universes that never happened. The couple keeps coming back to it.

**The story they tell at the next wedding.** "At our wedding, guests voted on which first dance we'd perform — and we actually rehearsed all three." That's the outcome. That's what $2,000 buys.

## Price Reframe

Don't defend the price. Reframe the category.

> Flowers: $800. Dead by morning.
> Photographer: $3,500. Guests don't see it for 6 weeks.
> Caterer: $5,000+. Forgotten by Tuesday.
> **Wepho: $2,000. Every guest participates. You keep it forever. They talk about it for years.**

The wedding budget mindset is already in "this is worth it" mode. $2,000 is a line item, not a sacrifice, for the couple in the ICP. The question they're actually asking is not "is this expensive?" but "is this worth the slot?"

## Brand Voice

**The tone:** Warm. Specific. Confident without being loud. Like a brilliant friend who happens to build things.

**Not:** Corporate. Cheesy wedding-speak. Overpromising. Generic positivity.

**The one rule for copy:** Specificity creates believability. Don't write "a trivia game about your relationship." Write "a trivia game where every question is about how you actually met, who proposed first, and what she said when he did."

**The contrast that does the work:** "Generic tools don't know who you are" is the implicit subtext of every headline. The specificity of Wepho's copy is itself proof of concept.

## Why This Wins

The wedding industry sells logistics. Photographers, caterers, venues, planners — all logistics, all about what happens at the wedding. Wepho sells experience and memory — what it *feels like* and what remains after.

No one else is in this slot. The generic tools (Kahoot, Google Forms, shared albums) don't know the couple. The planning tools (The Knot, Zola) are admin software. The photo apps capture moments but create nothing interactive.

Wepho is the only thing at the wedding that is simultaneously:
- Personalized to the couple at a deep level
- Interactive for every guest
- Live on the night
- Something the couple keeps forever

That combination is the moat. It can't be templated away — the value comes from the custom build.
